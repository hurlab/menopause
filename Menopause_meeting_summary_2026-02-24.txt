Objective and dataset framing (keep brief; one slide)
You analyzed GTEx adipose transcriptomic data using an updated age-binning scheme aligned to a pre/peri/post concept.
Current age bins used: <50 (pre), 50–60 (peri/middle), and 60+ (post/older). You noted this can be re-binned (e.g., 40–50 vs 60–70) if the group wants comparability to earlier analyses.

Key data structure issue: two tissues + two sequencing centers (batch/center confounding) (1–2 slides)
What you observed:
There are two adipose depots (subcutaneous and visceral), and samples come primarily from two sequencing centers.
The sequencing centers differ in sample counts and importantly in age distribution: one center has higher representation of younger subjects than the other, creating a confounding risk (center correlates with age).

What this implies:
This is not “pure technical batch” because the technical factor (center) is entangled with biology (age distribution). This makes aggressive batch correction risky.

Proposed handling options (you explained both; 1 slide):
Approach A (typical batch correction): normalize expression so centers match at baseline (e.g., SVA-like correction), then run differential analyses.
Risk: may erase true biological signals, especially when age distributions differ across centers.
Approach B (covariate adjustment): keep expression as-is, include sequencing center as a covariate in the model.
Benefit: reduces risk of removing biological signal; more conservative given center–age confounding.
You used Approach B for this analysis.

Optional additional safeguard you mentioned:
Exclude the youngest age strata entirely (e.g., 20–29 and 30–39) to reduce imbalance across centers, but you have not run this yet.

Gene-set scoring strategy (core analytic method) (1 slide)
The central question: for a given gene set, are its member genes systematically shifted up/down across samples in a condition/group?
Method summary:
For each gene, compute standardized expression relative to its distribution (z-score logic).
Aggregate across genes in a gene set to get an average z-score (gene-set “signal”).
Test whether that gene-set average differs across age groups vs what would be expected by chance.
This is conceptually aligned with z-score heatmap scaling, but used here as a quantitative, comparable signal across gene sets.

SEPARATE SUMMARY (cb1116): INTERPRETING LIPOLYSIS GENE/PROTEIN CHANGES + CATECHOLAMINE RESISTANCE

Core message: lower mRNA for “lipolysis genes” does not imply lower lipolysis.
cb1116 strongly cautioned against concluding that decreased expression of lipolytic genes (ATGL/PNPLA2, HSL/LIPE, etc.) means reduced lipolytic flux. Lipolysis is primarily regulated acutely and functionally through protein phosphorylation and signaling dynamics, not by transcriptional abundance alone.

Why decreased lipolysis-gene expression could happen when lipolysis is high (the paradox)
In chronic states of elevated sympathetic activation (classic example: obesity; possibly also long-term metabolic stress), tissues can develop catecholamine resistance (reduced responsiveness to adrenergic stimulation).
A hallmark mechanism is down-regulation of adrenergic receptors (and/or desensitization of downstream signaling), which reduces signaling despite high sympathetic tone.

How this relates to what you’re seeing
Your human subcutaneous analysis shows a downward transcriptional shift in a “lipolysis core gene set” with age/post-menopause groupings.
cb1116 argued that this pattern may be more intuitively interpreted as an adaptive/chronic-response signature consistent with catecholamine resistance, rather than a direct readout of reduced lipolysis.

Protein-level nuance (why mRNA and proteins can both mislead if interpreted simplistically)
cb1116 referenced that, in their long-standing high-fat diet context, protein abundance of lipolytic proteins can be reduced, which seems paradoxical if one assumes lipolysis has been chronically elevated.
This reduction in lipolytic protein expression could itself be a signature or consequence of catecholamine resistance (receptor down-regulation → reduced signaling → adaptive downshift in pathway components), but cb1116 emphasized they have not systematically proven this across the board.

Which genes are most informative for catecholamine resistance (per cb1116 + Kenichi discussion)
Adrenergic receptor genes, especially beta-adrenergic receptors, are the most established transcriptional indicators:
cb1116 highlighted beta-2 initially, then the discussion converged on beta-3 being strongly down-regulated in adipose (with beta-2 also relevant in Kenichi’s data).
Mechanistic rationale: reduced receptor expression → reduced receptor protein → reduced adrenergic signaling → contributes to catecholamine resistance.
For downstream lipolysis genes (ATGL/HSL etc.), the relationship to catecholamine resistance is less direct and less “diagnostic.”

Practical implications for your next analysis/presentation
When presenting “lipolysis gene-set down,” phrase it as:
A transcriptional downshift in lipolysis-related genes with aging/post-menopause that may reflect altered adrenergic tone/signaling adaptation (potential catecholamine resistance), rather than reduced lipolysis per se.
Immediately pair it with the planned follow-up:
Explicit receptor-level analysis (ADRB1/2/3; consider separating alpha vs beta receptor sets).
Downstream signaling proxies (cAMP/PKA-related gene sets).
If available, integrate protein/phosphorylation evidence (because phosphorylation is closer to functional lipolysis control).
